W6: Implementation

Partnership & Stakeholder Dynamics

Dr Lukas Wallrich (l.wallrich@bbk.ac.uk)

  • Implementation phase: where recommendations meet reality
  • This is where most consultancy value is realised or lost
  • Two things matter above all: partnership quality and stakeholder management
Core Principle
The ultimate goal of consulting is to make the consultant unnecessary.

— paraphrasing Peter Block, Flawless Consulting

  • Fundamental tension: organisations hire consultants to fill a gap
  • Temptation is to fill it directly — but wiser to enable the client
  • Best outcome: when the client says “we did it ourselves”
  • If change only works while you’re in the room, you haven’t really changed anything

Capability vs. Dependency

Dependency

  • Client waits for consultant input
  • Consultant does work clients could learn
  • Client knowledge isn’t increasing
  • Consultant involvement growing

Capability

  • Client staff lead meetings
  • They explain the approach to others
  • Steps implemented without checking in
  • Consultant involvement declining
⚠

The test: If you disappeared tomorrow, what would happen?

  • “Everything stops” = dependency; “they’d manage” = capability
  • Question isn’t whether to take on tasks — sometimes you should
  • Question is whether the client is learning or outsourcing

Remember the Monkey

Tasks migrate from the person who should own them to whoever picks them up.

During implementation, this accelerates:

  • The consultant has time when client staff are busy
  • The consultant has expertise clients feel they lack
  • It’s easier for everyone in the short term
💡

Every monkey that migrates to the consultant is a capability that doesn’t develop in the client.

  • Callback to Oncken & Wass’s “monkey on the shoulder” from Week 2
  • Every implementation activity should pass the test: “is this building their ability to continue without me?”
  • Practicalities of handover covered in Week 7 — but mindset starts now

The Role Shift During Implementation

Plumber

Expert pair-of-hands. Valuable but creates dependency if you stay here.

Doctor

Diagnose and prescribe. Dominant during analysis.

Advisor

Strategic partner. Key for solution design.

Navigator →

Guide through change. The implementation role.

  • Recall the four roles from Week 1
  • During diagnosis: Doctor or Advisor mode
  • Implementation demands Navigator: guiding through obstacles
  • Danger: getting stuck as Plumber — feels productive but creates dependency
  • Implementation demands a shift towards Navigator: helping clients steer through obstacles, delays, and resistance

From Stakeholders
to Partnership

The stakeholder map you created during diagnosis? It’s already out of date.

  • Transition: so far we focused on the consultant-client partnership
  • Implementation doesn’t happen in a bubble — touches many people
  • The diagnosis stakeholder map is already outdated

How Stakeholder
Positions Shift

  • Abstract supporters face concrete disruption
  • New stakeholders emerge as areas are affected
  • Power dynamics shift with resources
  • Early success can turn resisters into supporters
💡

Stakeholder mapping isn’t a one-time activity. It needs continuous attention.

  • “Yes, we need to restructure” is easy when theoretical — different when your team is reorganised
  • New stakeholders appear; power dynamics shift
  • Some sceptics come round when they see the approach is thoughtful
  • Continuous attention required throughout implementation

Sustaining the Five Beliefs

The challenge shifts from creating these beliefs to sustaining them.

Belief Initial Challenge Ongoing Challenge
Discrepancy Is change necessary? Is it still necessary?
Appropriateness Is this the right change? Is it still the right change?
Efficacy Can we do this? Can we keep doing this?
Principal support Are leaders committed? Are they still committed?
Valence What’s in it for me? Is it worth the ongoing cost?
⚠

Most vulnerable:
Efficacy (doubt grows when progress is slow) and
Principal support (leaders shift attention to the next initiative).

Based on Armenakis, Harris & Mossholder (1993)

  • Recall Armenakis’s five beliefs from Weeks 4–5
  • Implementation fatigue erodes these beliefs over time
  • Efficacy most vulnerable: early enthusiasm fades when implementation gets hard
  • Principal support fragile: leaders great at launching, less good at staying committed through the “boring middle”

Implementation vs. Design Resistance

Design Resistance

About the idea

  • Questioning whether the problem is real
  • Disputing the diagnosis
  • Challenging the proposed solution

Response: persuasion

Implementation Resistance

About the disruption

  • Already overloaded with other work
  • Timing conflicts with peak season
  • New way harder to learn than expected

Response: support and motivation

💡

Key distinction: “I agree this is the right thing, but I can’t do it right now” is not resistance to overcome;
it’s (often) a resource or timing problem to solve.

  • Nature of resistance changes during implementation
  • Design resistance = persuasion problem; implementation resistance = support problem
  • Treating implementation resistance as a persuasion problem misses the point entirely

💭 Think about a change initiative you’ve experienced. Was your stakeholder map static or did it evolve? What happened to stakeholder positions as implementation progressed?
