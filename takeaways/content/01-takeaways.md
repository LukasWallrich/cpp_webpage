## Looking back: lessons to take away

We've covered a lot of ground this term - from what consultancy even is, through contracting, diagnosis, designing solutions, implementation, and evaluation ... alongside conversations about business development, personal growth, AI and ethics. Here are the lessons I'd most want you to carry forward, if *I* got to choose. Not as a definitive list, but as the things I keep coming back to when I think about what makes consulting work well, and what makes consultants effective.

### Doing consultancy well

These are about the craft of individual engagements - how you work with clients, how you think about problems, and how you deliver value.

#### 1. Personal relationships matter

Clients hire firms, but they work with people. The brand on the slide deck might get you through the door, but what happens after that depends on how individuals connect. This applies at every stage: the initial pitch, the awkward first interviews where people aren't sure why you're there, the difficult conversation when your findings aren't what anyone wanted to hear, the presentation where you're asking people to change how they work. I hope you got a taste of that during our Saturday workshop.

It's easy to focus on the technical agenda - the data, the analysis, the recommendation - because that feels like the "real" work. But every interaction also has an interpersonal dimension. Are you building trust or eroding it? Are people telling you what they actually think, or what they think you want to hear? Do they see you as someone who's there to help, or someone who's there to judge?

This doesn't mean being artificially warm or spending half your time on small talk. It means paying attention to how people experience working with you, and recognising that the quality of those relationships directly shapes the quality (and impact) of your work.

#### 2. Build client ownership, not dependency

Block's [-@block2011] central insight runs through the entire module: the goal of consulting is to make yourself unnecessary. That sounds counterintuitive as a business proposition, but it's both the ethical foundation and, in the long run, the practical one.

Remember the lottery ticket study from Week 4-5? People value outcomes more when they've had a hand in creating them. The same applies to consulting recommendations. A solution the client has co-designed is one they understand, believe in, and can sustain after you leave. A solution you hand them on a silver platter - however brilliant - is one they may not fully understand and almost certainly won't maintain.

This connects to Oncken & Wass's [-@oncken1999] "who's got the monkey?" question I kept returning to. Every time you take ownership of a task the client could do, you're training them to depend on you. Every time you enable them to do it themselves, you're building capability. The worst outcome isn't a failed engagement - it's a "successful" one where the client can't function without you, so they hire you again to do the same thing ... until they give up on consultants for good.

#### 3. Plan for implementation from day one

Professional values aside, most of us go into consulting because we want to see things actually change. Clients are also increasingly unwilling to pay for reports that gather dust. But the gap between recommendation and reality is where most engagements fail - not because the analysis was wrong, but because nobody planned seriously for what would happen after the presentation.

Implementation isn't a phase that comes after the "real" work of diagnosis and design. It's something you need to think about from contracting onwards. Who will own the change internally? What capabilities need to be built? What will make people resist, and how do you design for that rather than being surprised by it? If you can't answer those questions, your beautifully constructed recommendation is just expensive wallpaper. And sometimes this requires difficult conversations with the client - if they don't want to pay for implementation, they better be prepared to invest substantial (dedicated) internal resources that you can hand over to.

#### 4. Embrace experimentation and learning cycles

Consultants are often brought in for wicked problems - situations where nobody fully understands the issue, where cause and effect are tangled, where the "right answer" depends on context and changes over time. Treating these like technical problems with linear solutions (i.e. a step-by-step consultancy cycle) is risky.

Where it's possible, run actual experiments. Build minimum viable products and prototypes to get feedback early, before you've invested too much in a direction that might be wrong. Plan pilots to test implementation approaches before rolling them out across an organisation. Use approaches like action learning and positive deviance that treat the people closest to the problem as sources of solutions, not just recipients of your expertise.

None of this means abandoning structure or rigour. It means being honest about what you don't know and designing ways to learn as you go, rather than pretending you can see the whole path from the start. Sometimes that makes proposal writing harder, but it is worth it.

#### 5. Treat resistance as data

When people push back on your work - during interviews, in workshops, on recommendations - the instinct is to treat it as an obstacle to overcome. Schein [-@schein1987] reframes this: resistance is information. It tells you something about what matters to people, what they're afraid of losing, where the real dynamics lie.

Someone who resists a reorganisation might be protecting their team, or might have seen three previous reorganisations fail. Someone who won't share data might be worried about how it will be used, not hiding anything sinister. Someone who challenges your methodology in a meeting might be the most engaged person in the room.

This doesn't mean all resistance is legitimate or that you should abandon your recommendations at the first sign of pushback. It means slowing down to understand what's driving it. Week 6 distinguished between design resistance (people have different priorities, or aren't convinced yet) and implementation resistance (the change genuinely disrupts something important). They require very different responses - and lumping them together usually makes things worse.

#### 6. Ground your work in evidence

Consultants are brought in to help clients do things they can't do themselves. Process consultation and facilitation matter, but clients also need you to bring substantive knowledge. That knowledge can come from many places: academic literature, business case studies, industry best practices, expert interviews, client stakeholders, and your own (ever-growing) experience.

All of these sources are of mixed quality and mixed transferability. The evidence-based practice framework from Week 4-5 - ask, acquire, appraise, aggregate, apply, assess - is a useful discipline for being systematic about this. Not every engagement needs a literature review, but none of them should be based purely on gut feeling and *uncritically* recycled frameworks from your last project.

The honest challenge is that evidence in management consulting is rarely as clean as evidence in medicine (and even medical evidence is messier than people think). Context matters enormously. What worked spectacularly at one organisation may fail at another. Being evidence-informed means holding that complexity honestly, neither pretending certainty you don't have nor claiming that *everything* is down to context, and thus resisting learning.

#### 7. Build a personal toolkit of frameworks

It's easy to dismiss frameworks as performative consultant jargon - and some of them are. But a few simple ones are genuinely useful as ways to sense-check and structure thinking on tasks that come up again and again.

When thinking about getting people to change, then the influence model (role modelling, conviction, skills, formal mechanisms) is a simple way to make sure you're not leaving out something obvious. When understanding an initial situation, then SWOT, for all its simplicity, forces you to think about internal and external factors together. When diagnosing problems, then root cause analysis and issue trees help you move past symptoms to causes.

The key is having a few of these in your personal toolbox and using them enough in everyday work to know which ones *you* actually find useful. More comprehensive frameworks - PESTLE, the Cultural Web, McKinsey's 7S - can be helpful but also risk 'boiling the ocean'. Also, no client is impressed by you bringing a textbook framework to the table as though it's a revelation. The framework should usually be invisible scaffolding for your thinking, not the centrepiece of your deliverable (which was very well done in this year's group assignments).

#### 8. Take ethics personally

Consultancy is not a regulated profession. There is no equivalent of being struck off the medical register or disbarred. Professional body guidelines exist, but they have limited enforcement power. O'Mahoney [-@omahoney2011] calls this "ethical individualisation" - the moral responsibility falls on you as an individual, even when contradictory pressures come from your firm, your client, or the commercial reality of winning the next contract.

This means thinking through your boundaries before you're under pressure. Who will you work for, and who won't you? Where do you draw the line between serving the client's stated interests and serving what you believe is right? How do you handle the tension between what the person who hired you wants to hear and what the organisation actually needs? What do you do when a client asks you to validate a decision they've already made, especially but not only if you think it's a poor decision?

There are no universal answers to these questions ... but people who have thought about them in advance tend to handle them better than those who encounter them for the first time in the moment, under pressure, with a fee at stake.

### Being effective as a consultant

These three are less about any single engagement and more about how you develop and sustain yourself as a professional over time.

#### 9. Practise reflection - and go beyond the surface

Argyris [-@argyris1991] identified something awkward: the people who are best at single-loop learning (refining their technique, getting more efficient at what they already do) are often the worst at double-loop learning (questioning whether they're doing the right thing in the first place). High-performing professionals tend to have a lot invested in being competent, which makes it uncomfortable to ask "what am I not seeing?" or "why did I frame the problem this way?"

But that deeper reflection is where the most valuable learning happens. A post-engagement review that only asks "what could we have done more efficiently?" is single-loop. One that asks "did we solve the right problem?" or "whose interests did our recommendations actually serve?" is double-loop. The second kind is harder, less comfortable, and often more useful - especially when the world is changing, as it is at the moment due to AI (among other factors).

Schön's [-@schon1983] distinction between reflection-in-action (thinking on your feet during an engagement) and reflection-on-action (looking back afterwards) is worth keeping in mind too. Both matter. The consultant who never pauses to think during a client meeting will miss signals; the consultant who never looks back after a project will repeat mistakes. Building regular reflection into your practice - whether through journalling, peer discussion, supervision, or just a quiet hour with a coffee after a difficult week - is one of the highest-return investments you can make.

#### 10. Diversify and commodify strategically

In a world being disrupted - by AI, by shifts in what clients are willing to pay for, by new competitors - having exactly one offering is risky. One of consulting's great advantages is that clients pay you to develop knowledge you can then, to some extent, package and reuse. Every engagement is an opportunity to build expertise that has value beyond that specific project.

This might mean developing a methodology you can license, writing about your field to build reputation, creating training programmes based on your consulting experience, or building diagnostic tools that open doors to larger engagements. The point isn't to try everything - focus and brand identity matter - but to think creatively about how your knowledge can work for you in more than one way. (In that, Sasha's experience is highly pertinent to me - who would have thought that one of the fastest growing sectors of consulting in 2020 would essentially be shut down in 2025?)

But this is not always easy. Heusinkveld & Benders [-@heusinkveld2005] showed that commodification is a political process, not just a technical one. Packaging knowledge involves choices about what gets captured and what gets lost, who benefits from reuse, and whether your product actually fits the problems it claims to solve. So this needs to be done thoughtfully, not mechanically.

#### 11. Engage seriously with AI

This one has changed faster than any of us anticipated, and it will keep changing. A recent [article in Nature](https://www.nature.com/articles/d41586-026-00285-6) argues that we functionally already reached Artificial General Intelligence (AGI), which was expected to be a major turning point. In any case, AI is already raising the floor on what acceptable consulting outputs look like - a well-structured analysis, a competent literature review, a polished slide deck are increasingly table stakes when an AI can produce a reasonable first draft in minutes.

That has several implications. It makes the distinctly human parts of consulting more important, not less: reading a room, building trust, navigating politics, exercising judgement in ambiguous situations. It raises new questions about knowledge management - when a RAG system can search every report your firm has ever produced, what does that mean for how you capture and structure knowledge? It also raises ethical questions that the profession hasn't fully grappled with: transparency about AI use, responsibility for AI-generated analysis, the risk of homogenised thinking when everyone is drawing on the same models, and the responsibility to give junior consultants opportunities for growth even if their work does not reach the bar set by AI from day 1.

The people I've seen handle this well aren't the ones who either ignore AI entirely or delegate everything to it. They're the ones who engage with it seriously - understanding what it can and can't do, using it to augment rather than replace their thinking, and being honest with clients about its role. It's not something any of us can afford to ignore, even if we're still figuring out exactly where the boundaries should be.
